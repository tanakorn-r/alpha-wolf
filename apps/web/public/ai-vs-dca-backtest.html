AI investing strategy versus normal monthly DCA

AlphaWolf AI Replay is built to show the full trade-off—not to make the Agent win. Normal DCA can finish with more money, while an Agent plan may hold cash and reduce drawdown.

How the comparison works

The replay contributes the same scheduled cash to both approaches. Normal DCA invests each contribution on schedule. The Agent can buy, add, hold cash, trim, or sell according to its character and the evidence available at that historical decision point. The replay avoids deliberately using future outcomes in earlier decisions.

Dividends are accumulated

Dividend payments are included in ending value and return. The Agent receives dividends on the shares it actually owns and can retain them in the cash pool for later buys. Normal DCA receives and reinvests its dividends according to the benchmark rules.

Average exposure is not ending cash

Average exposure measures how much of available capital was invested across the whole replay. Ending cash reserve measures only the unused cash at the final date. A plan can average 51% exposure and still end with 22% cash because exposure changed month by month.

Return is contribution-adjusted

The plan return is calculated from the actual simulated account value and contributions. It is not simply “25% deployed × the stock return.” Each contribution, buy, dividend, trim, price change, and remaining cash affects the path.

Why normal DCA often wins

During a strong rising market, staying invested captures more upside. A cautious Agent that waits for a cheaper entry may miss part of the rise. That is the real cost of downside protection. The useful comparison therefore includes both ending value and contribution-adjusted peak-to-trough drawdown.

What capital efficiency means

A scaled full-exposure estimate asks what return the Agent's observed performance might imply if the same result were proportionally sized to full exposure. It is a sizing estimate, not a simulated result. Full loading would also amplify losses and drawdown, so it should never be presented as guaranteed performance.
